Anomaly Detection

Anomaly detection is the process of finding anomalies, or outliers, in a given dataset. Anomalies may occur naturally due to rare processes. Anomalies may also occur due to abnormal or fraudulant behavior. For example, a credit card company may wish to perform anomaly detection on credit card transactions in order to limit fraud in case a credit card is stolen. This presents an issue: when is an event rare compared to being a true anomaly? In the case of the credit card company, when should the credit card be deactivated?

Statistical approaches

Statistics is the most straightforward way to detect anomalies. The mean and standard deviation, in addition to other possible summary statistics, are computed for each attribute of a set of data records. Using these values, it is possible to determine if a given data point falls outside of an acceptable tolerance range surrounding most of the data. These points are the anomalies.

This approach works well for datasets with a small number of dimensions, especially for attributes that are normally distributed. If these properties are violated, it may be less meaningful to use statistics without additional approcaches to determine outliers.

Proximity approaches

Proximity approaches to anomaly detection involve looking at close a given data point is to the nearest K neighbors. This can be directly found by running the k-nearest neighbor classifier and then finding the points that have the largest distances to the nearest neighbors.

Density approaches

A density-based approach to anomaly detection determines anomalous data points by finding records in areas of low density. The probability that a point is an anomaly is then inversly proportional to the density.

Clustering approaches

Clustering approaches to anomaly detection involve running clustering algorithms on datasets and then finding groups that are small or are far away from other groups. The points in these groups are likely to be anomalies.
